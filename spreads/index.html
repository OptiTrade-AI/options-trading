Put Credit Spread Deep Dive

What Is It?

A neutral-to-bullish strategy. Sell a higher-strike put and buy a lower-strike put (same expiration) to collect a net credit upfront.

Stock Selection

  • High-liquidity stocks/ETFs: SPY, QQQ, AAPL, MSFT, NVDA
  • Stable fundamentals & moderate/high implied volatility

Delta Selection

  • Short put: ~25–35 for probability edge
  • Long put: ~5–10 delta to cap max loss

Width & Premium Target

  • Common widths: $5 or $10 spreads
  • Aim for ≥20% of width as (e.g., $2 premium on $10 width)
  • Narrower widths → higher win rates, lower yield

Position Sizing & Risk

  • Risk ≤2–5% of total portfolio per trade
  • Max loss = (width – credit) × 100
  • Adjust contract count to stay within risk limits

Expiration & Exit Plan

  • Choose 30–45 for optimal time decay
  • Exit at 50%–75% of max profit
  • Close by 21 DTE regardless of P/L

Δ vs Width Trade-offs

Short answer: You can’t have both a $5-wide spread and a 30 delta/10 delta pairing at 30 DTE on NVDA—these don’t align. Adjust these three settings to balance your trade:

Setting Impact on Delta Impact on Risk
Strike Distance Wider strikes increase the delta gap (e.g., 30 to 10 delta). Increases spread width and max loss.
Time to Expiration Shorter time tightens delta gap for the same strikes. Increases , reduces time to adjust.
Delta Targets Choose closer deltas (e.g., 15–20 delta for long leg). Reduces spread width and max loss.

Key Takeaways

  • Put credit spreads offer defined risk and upfront premium.
  • Select liquid symbols and appropriate deltas (short ~30, long ~8).
  • Aim for credit ≥20% of width using $5–$10 spreads.
  • Risk ≤2–5% of portfolio and know your max loss.
  • Exit at 50% profit or by 21 DTE to maximize returns.

What is Delta?

Delta measures how much an option’s price changes for a $1 move in the underlying stock.

  • In put credit spreads, the short put’s delta (e.g., 25–35) indicates a 25–35% chance of expiring in-the-money.
  • The long put’s lower delta (e.g., 5–10) reduces risk by capping losses.
  • Higher delta = closer to the money, higher premium, higher risk.

What is Premium?

The premium is the amount you receive (credit) when selling a put credit spread.

  • It’s the net difference between the price of the sold put and the bought put.
  • Aiming for ≥20% of the spread width ensures a good risk-reward ratio.
  • Example: A $10 wide spread with a $2 credit gives 20% of the width.

What is DTE?

DTE stands for Days to Expiration, the time until an option contract expires.

  • Choosing 30–45 DTE balances time decay and flexibility.
  • Closing by 21 DTE avoids risks from rapid price movements near expiration.
  • Longer DTE = slower decay, shorter DTE = faster decay.

What is Gamma?

Gamma measures how quickly an option’s delta changes when the stock price moves.

  • Higher gamma means delta changes faster, making the trade more sensitive to price swings.
  • Shorter expiration times increase gamma, raising risk near expiration.
  • In put credit spreads, high gamma can lead to larger losses if the stock moves against you.
