What Are Options?

Options are contracts that give you the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset (like stocks) at a specific price by a certain date.

The "option" aspect means flexibility—you can choose to exercise the contract or let it expire. Unlike owning stocks, options don't give you ownership, just rights.

Analogy: Think of an option like a restaurant reservation. You secure a table (right), but you're not obligated to show up. If you do, you get the table at the agreed time; if not, you lose the booking fee.

Call Options Explained

A call option gives you the right to buy shares at a specific price, called the strike price, before the expiration date.

You pay a premium (cost of the option) for this right. Calls are profitable when the stock price rises above the strike price plus the premium.

Example: Apple is trading at $200. You buy a $210 call option for a $5 premium. If Apple rises to $250, you can buy shares at $210, making a profit (minus the premium). If it stays below $210, you lose the premium.

Put Options Explained

A put option gives you the right to sell shares at the strike price before the expiration date.

Puts are profitable when the stock price falls below the strike price minus the premium.

Example: Tesla is trading at $150. You buy a $145 put option for a $4 premium. If Tesla drops to $100, you can sell at $145, earning a profit. If it stays above $145, you lose the premium.

Options Terminology

  • Strike Price: The fixed price at which you can buy (call) or sell (put) the stock.
  • Premium: The cost of the option contract.
  • Expiration Date: The date the option contract expires.
  • In-the-Money (ITM): Call: stock price >ariat strike price; Put: stock price < strike price.
  • Out-of-the-Money (OTM): Call: stock price < strike price; Put: stock price > strike price.
  • At-the-Money (ATM): Stock price ≈ strike price.

Basic Options Math

Call Break-Even: Strike Price + Premium

Put Break-Even: Strike Price - Premium

Maximum Profit:

  • Call: Unlimited (as stock price rises)
  • Put: Strike Price - Premium (if stock price → $0)

Maximum Risk: The premium paid for the option.

Common Beginner Mistakes

  • Ignoring Implied Volatility: High volatility increases premiums, affecting profitability.
  • Not Understanding Time Decay: Options lose value as expiration nears.
  • Buying Far OTM Options: Cheap but unlikely to profit.
